House Rules - General

Ascending Armor Class

We will be using ascending Armor Class for this campaign rather than AD&D 2nd Edition's default of descending armor class. In this method, armor class starts at 10 and goes up. The necessary impact of this is that THAC0 cannot be used, and instead the character will have a positive number as a to-hit bonus.

To adjust the character to fit this model, first calculate your character's THAC0 and AC as given in the rules. Then simply subtract each from 20, which will give you your to-hit bonus and your ascending AC that will be used in the game.

For example, assume that you have a character with a THAC0 of 17 with their long sword and an AC of 5 (chain mail & shield). To modify these, you would subtract each from 20, giving your character a +3 to hit with the long sword, and an AC of 15. Thus when your character attacks, you would roll 1d20 and add +3 to the result. For someone to get past your armor, they would have to roll a 15 or better on 1d20, modified by their own to-hit bonuses.

Bringing in New Characters

There are a number of reasons why a player may want to bring in a new character. Perhaps they have found their current character to be not to their liking. Or their character has died and cannot be raised. In such cases where a player brings in another character, that character will start off with 0 xp.

Professional Guilds

While PC’s may find tutors however they wish, some may find benefit in joining professional guilds. Membership in these organizations may grant any number of benefits, one of which is to assist in finding and paying for training. Basic membership in such a professional guild is generally easy to acquire, but more benefit will accrue to members who demonstrate their loyalty to the guild and advance in rank. Note that a character can belong to more than one guild, but they can only ever advance in one guild. Further, belonging to guilds that are in opposition will inevitably draw the ire of these guilds resulting in expulsion or worse.

Professional guilds generally have five tiers of rank, as given below (the title will differ from guild to guild):

Tier Training Discount Minimum Level Benefits/Notes
Member 0% 1 Can find trainers
Dedicated 10% 3 Free room & board
Proven 25% 6 May command others of lower rank
Overseer 50% 9 Leads smaller branches
Leader 50% 12 Carries great influence among the organization

Note that the Miniminum Level specified above represent only that, the minimum level that a character must be to advance. However, each organization will also have other requirements that a character must meet, so advancement upon reaching the appropriate level is not automatic.

Mechanically, membership in a guild is represented using nonweapon proficiencies (see NonWeapon Proficiencies above). A character may start the campaign with a membership in one or more guilds if they so desire. However, a character cannot gain a rank of Proven in more than two guilds, and a character cannot be members of two or more guilds that are in opposition. When a character achieves a higher rank, their next nonweapon proficiency must be used for the guild. Thus, for example, a character of the Dedicated rank will have purchased the guild nonweapon prociency twice (getting the +1 bonus on any guild-related skill rolls); and they will have purchased it five times if they achieve the rank of Leader (again, getting a +4 to any guild-related skill rolls).

Spell Components

Common spell components less than 10 gp in value do not have to be tracked by the player. We will assume that the character gathers a sufficient quantity of the required spell components in his down time.

Purchasing Magic Items

As a general rule, magic items must be earned, not purchased. This does not absolutely preclude PC’s from purchasing magic items, but such purchases will generally be one-off occurrences. There are no magic shops. Players may also contract with powerful wizards to produce magic items for them, but they will have to take the initiative themselves to work out terms with the wizard.

Potions of healing may be acquired at most temples for 200 gp, though especially for small temples their inventory may be minimal. One does not need to be of the temple's faith to purchase these potions, but a temple will refuse to sell potions to individuals they know or suspect to be of contrary intent (e.g., a temple of Tyr won't sell potions to someone they know to treat unjustly with others).

Paladins and priests can also often acquire scrolls of healing as well for 200 gp. Druids may acquire such scrolls as well, but at a cost of 250 gp. Other scrolls may sometimes be acquired, but these are far more rare and more expensive. As with potions of healing, the purchaser does not need to be of the specific faith of the temple, but they cannot be in opposition.

Wizards will sometimes sell scrolls and potions as well, as some non-adventuring wizards support themselves by producing such magics. However, there is no regular trade in such things, so PC’s will have to seek out wizards on their own, and there is no guarantee that a wizard will have the potion/scroll they are looking for or even any potion or scroll at all.

Creating Scrolls and Potions

The rules as given on pp. 118-120 of the Dungeon Master's Guide will be used, with the following exceptions:

  • Wizards and priests may create scrolls and potions starting at 1st level, except for protection scrolls which still can only be created by a wizard of 9th level or greater.
  • Lower levels scrolls and potions will generally require cheaper and more readily accessible components. For example, to pen a scroll, the Dungeon Master's Guide states, "...the pen can't be just from an ordinary goose quill. It must be from a strange and magical creature..." However, for lower level scrolls, a goose quill may work just fine, albeit, a fresh unused one. Similarly, other required elements may be easily acquired.
  • A wizard may brew a potion in a laboratory costing less than 2,000 gp. For every 100 gp below this cost, 2% is subtracted from the chance of success. For every 500 gp above this, 2% is added to the chance of success.

Secret and Concealed Doors for Non-Elves

Although elves and half-elves have special abilities to find secret and concealed doors, non-elves can also search for hidden doors. The chance for success for all non-elves is the same, one step lower on a d6 than elves or half-elves. That is, when actively searching, a non-elf can find a secret door on 1 in 6 chance, and they can find a concealed door on a 2 in 6 chance. They can never find a concealed door simply by passing by it as elves and half-elves can.
